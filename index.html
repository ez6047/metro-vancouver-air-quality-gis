PM2.5 and Potential Source Land Use at the 1 km Scale

An ArcGIS Pro analysis of whether land-cover classes that could plausibly contribute to PM2.5 are spatially associated with higher fine particulate matter concentrations across Metro Vancouver.

Course: GEOS 370 Software: ArcGIS Pro Study Area: Metro Vancouver

Project Snapshot

  • Remote-sensed PM2.5 raster surface
  • Land-cover raster reclassified into source/non-source categories
  • Thiessen polygon and tabulated-area workflow
  • OLS and GWR models used to compare global and local relationships

1. Home

Abstract

This project examines whether the percentage of land cover classified as potential PM2.5-source land use is spatially associated with elevated PM2.5 concentrations across Metro Vancouver. The analysis uses two main datasets: a Metro Vancouver land-cover classification raster and a 2023 annual ground-level PM2.5 raster from the Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group at Washington University in St. Louis, provided at 0.01° × 0.01° resolution (about 1 km × 1 km). Land-cover classes were reclassified into binary source and non-source categories, while PM2.5 raster cells were converted to points and then Thiessen polygons so that surrounding land-cover composition could be summarized consistently.

The final modelling layer was created by clipping PM2.5 polygons to the reclassified land-use polygons, tabulating area by class, calculating the percentage of each polygon covered by potential source land use, and joining that value back to the PM2.5 polygons. Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression and geographically weighted regression (GWR) were then used to compare the global and local spatial relationships between potential source land use and PM2.5.

The results show a positive but limited global relationship. The OLS model produced an R² of 0.201, meaning that percent potential source land use explained about 20.1% of the spatial variation in PM2.5, while the coefficient of 0.018248 indicates that each one-percentage-point increase in potential source land use was associated with a 0.018248-unit increase in PM2.5. GWR showed that this relationship varies across space and improved local interpretation, but strong residual clustering remained. Overall, the project achieved its goal of identifying a spatial association between land use and PM2.5, while also showing that land use alone does not fully explain PM2.5 patterns across Metro Vancouver.

2. Introduction

Urban Air Quality and Land-Cover Patterns

Air quality is a central urban environmental issue because it influences respiratory health, cardiovascular risk, and overall quality of life. Fine particulate matter, especially PM2.5, is commonly used as an indicator because these particles are small enough to remain suspended in the air and penetrate deeply into the lungs.

Urban land-cover patterns can influence PM2.5 exposure because built surfaces, paved areas, industrial activity, transportation corridors, and other intensive land uses often cluster in particular parts of a metropolitan region. Metro Vancouver is a useful study area because it contains dense urban cores, suburban development, agricultural areas, industrial lands, water bodies, and large vegetated areas within a relatively compact region.

Why GIS for This Question?

GIS makes it possible to align raster and vector datasets, reclassify land-cover categories, summarize area by polygon, and visualize spatial patterns that would be difficult to interpret from tables alone.

3. Project Objective

Research Focus

Main Research Question

Is the percentage of potential PM2.5-source land use spatially associated with higher PM2.5 concentrations across Metro Vancouver?

Project Goals

  • Reclassify the land-cover raster into potential PM2.5 source and non-source categories.
  • Convert PM2.5 raster cells into Thiessen polygons for area-based comparison.
  • Calculate the percentage of each PM2.5 polygon covered by potential source land use.
  • Use OLS and GWR to compare global and local relationships between land-use composition and PM2.5.
  • Evaluate residual spatial autocorrelation using Global Moran’s I.

4. Data

Input Datasets

Land-use raster

This raster provides the categorical land-cover surface used to identify where potential PM2.5-source land uses are concentrated across Metro Vancouver. The map shows a clear contrast between dense urban and industrial land covers in the Vancouver, Burnaby, Richmond, Surrey, and Langley corridor and the more vegetated and mountainous land covers in the northern part of the region. That contrast is important because the analysis later simplifies these detailed classes into binary source and non-source categories.

In practical terms, this dataset is the explanatory input. It does not measure emissions directly. Instead, it acts as a land-use proxy for surfaces and land-cover types that could plausibly be associated with higher particulate matter production or accumulation.

Data source: Metro Vancouver Open Data land-use raster

PM2.5 raster

This surface represents the dependent variable in the analysis: annual ground-level fine particulate matter for 2023. The dataset was produced by the Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group at Washington University in St. Louis and is provided at 0.01° × 0.01° resolution, which is approximately 1 km × 1 km. It estimates PM2.5 by combining satellite-derived aerosol optical depth observations, the GEOS-Chem chemical transport model, and calibration to ground-based monitoring observations.

Because the raster is a regional gridded surface, it is appropriate for neighbourhood- to regional-scale comparison rather than street-level exposure analysis. The map captures broad spatial gradients in PM2.5 across Metro Vancouver, which makes it suitable for testing whether areas with more potential source land use also tend to have higher concentrations.

Data source: Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group, Washington University in St. Louis — Surface PM2.5 V6.GL.02.04

5. Methodology

ArcGIS Workflow

Workflow Design

The two main inputs were not directly comparable in their original forms. The land-cover dataset was a categorical raster, while the PM2.5 surface was a continuous raster of yearly mean concentration values. The workflow therefore converted both datasets into a shared polygon-based analytical structure so that each PM2.5 zone could be assigned a corresponding percentage of land classified as a potential PM2.5 source. The final output was a polygon layer in which each feature contained both a PM2.5 value and an explanatory variable representing the proportion of nearby land use coded as a potential source.

This workflow was designed to preserve the original PM2.5 grid values while translating the land-cover classification into a simpler binary variable suitable for regression analysis. Each geoprocessing step moved the project from raw raster inputs toward a final modelling layer that could support OLS regression, GWR, and residual spatial autocorrelation testing.

Percent potential source land use = (area coded as potential PM2.5 source / total land-use area in the polygon) × 100

Step 1: Reclassify Land Use Raster

The original land-cover raster contained multiple classes, including built surfaces, vegetation types, water, and mixed land-cover categories. These classes were reclassified using the criteria table shown in the workflow figure so that the dataset was reduced to a binary source/non-source surface. A value of 0 represented land-cover classes not treated as potential PM2.5 sources, while a value of 1 represented classes treated as potential PM2.5 source land use. This step was necessary because the original categorical raster was too detailed to use directly as a single explanatory variable in regression analysis.

Step 2: Convert Raster to Polygon

After reclassification, the binary land-use raster was converted to polygons. This converted the source and non-source categories into vector features with explicit boundaries, which made later overlay and area calculations possible. Polygon conversion was important because the eventual objective was to measure how much of each PM2.5 analysis unit was covered by land classified as a potential source.

Step 3: Convert PM2.5 Raster to Points

The PM2.5 raster was then converted to points so that each raster cell became a point feature carrying its PM2.5 value. This preserved the original concentration information while creating a dataset that could be used to build Thiessen polygons. Converting the raster directly to points avoided averaging neighbouring cells and maintained the original PM2.5 value associated with each raster location.

Step 4: Convert PM2.5 Points to Thiessen Polygons

Thiessen polygons were created from the PM2.5 points so that each point defined the surrounding area closest to that PM2.5 value. This transformed the continuous raster surface into discrete polygon units that could be intersected with the land-use polygons. The Thiessen method preserved local PM2.5 representation while producing areal units suitable for tabulating land-cover area.

Step 5: Clip PM2.5 Polygon to Land Use Polygon

The PM2.5 Thiessen polygons were clipped to the reclassified land-use polygon extent so that both datasets occupied the same spatial footprint. This ensured that later area summaries were only calculated where both PM2.5 and land-use information existed. Clipping also prepared the data for tabulation by removing areas outside the overlapping study extent.

Step 6: Use Tabulate Area to Summarize Land-Use Area

The Tabulate Area tool was used to calculate the total area of land coded as non-source and the total area coded as potential source within each clipped PM2.5 polygon. This step converted the binary land-use surface into numeric area totals for each analysis unit, making it possible to compare PM2.5 concentration against local land-cover composition.

Step 7: Use Calculate Field to Compute Percent Potential Source Land Use

The tabulated area totals were then used to calculate the percentage of each PM2.5 polygon classified as potential PM2.5-source land use. This percentage was the main explanatory variable used in the regression analysis. It represents the share of the polygon area occupied by land-cover classes coded as plausible PM2.5 source land use.

Step 8: Join Tabulated Table with Clipped PM2.5 Polygon

Finally, the tabulated area and percentage fields were joined back to the clipped PM2.5 polygon layer. The resulting feature class contained both the PM2.5 value and the percentage of potential source land use for each polygon. This final modelling layer was then used for mapping, OLS regression, GWR analysis, and residual spatial autocorrelation testing.

6. Results

Maps and Model Outputs

OLS Model Interpretation

Global R² = 0.201

The percent potential-source land-use variable explains approximately 20.1% of the observed variation in PM2.5 across the analysis polygons.

Coefficient = 0.018248

Each 1 percentage point increase in potential PM2.5-source land use is associated with a 0.018248 µg/m³ increase in PM2.5.

The OLS model shows a positive global relationship between potential PM2.5-source land use and PM2.5 concentration. An R² of 0.201 means that the land-use percentage variable explains about one-fifth of the variation in PM2.5 values. This is a meaningful but limited relationship: most PM2.5 variation remains unexplained by this single predictor, which suggests that other factors such as meteorology, topography, traffic volume, industrial emissions, regional background pollution, and atmospheric transport also influence the observed PM2.5 surface.

The coefficient of 0.018248 means that, on average, a 1 percentage point increase in land use classified as a potential PM2.5 source corresponds to an increase of 0.018248 µg/m³ in PM2.5. Put another way, a 10 percentage point increase in potential source land use corresponds to an expected increase of approximately 0.18248 µg/m³. This coefficient should be interpreted as an association, not proof that the land-use classes directly caused the measured PM2.5 values.

Residual Spatial Autocorrelation

The OLS residuals show very strong positive spatial autocorrelation. The Global Moran’s I value is 0.957019, with a z-score of 76.098159 and a p-value reported as 0.000000. This means the OLS residuals are highly clustered rather than randomly distributed, so the global regression model does not fully account for the spatial structure in PM2.5.

The GWR residuals also remain spatially autocorrelated, but less strongly than the OLS residuals. The Global Moran’s I value decreases to 0.678473, with a z-score of 54.015470 and a p-value reported as 0.000000. This suggests that GWR captures some of the spatial non-stationarity in the relationship, but substantial spatial structure remains unexplained.

7. Discussion

Interpretation, Conclusions, and Limitations

The analysis identified a positive global association between potential PM2.5-source land use and PM2.5 concentration across Metro Vancouver. The OLS R² of 0.201 means that percent potential source land use explained about 20.1% of the observed spatial variation in PM2.5. This is meaningful because it shows the variable is related to PM2.5, but it is also limited because roughly 79.9% of the variation remains unexplained by this single predictor.

The OLS coefficient of 0.018248 indicates that, on average, a one-percentage-point increase in land classified as potential PM2.5-source area is associated with a 0.018248-unit increase in PM2.5. In practical terms, polygons containing more built, paved, industrial, or otherwise source-like land-cover classes tended to have somewhat higher PM2.5 values. This supports the project goal by showing that land-use composition is linked to particulate matter patterns, but it does not demonstrate direct causation.

GWR improves the interpretation by allowing the relationship to vary across space. The local model-fit surface shows that explanatory strength is not constant across Metro Vancouver, which indicates spatial non-stationarity in the relationship. In other words, potential source land use appears to matter more in some parts of the region than in others. However, the remaining GWR residual autocorrelation shows that even the local model does not fully account for the spatial structure in the PM2.5 surface.

Overall, the analyses achieved the main project goal: they identified a measurable spatial association between potential PM2.5-source land use and PM2.5 concentration. At the same time, the results also show that land use alone is not enough to explain the full regional pattern. Other factors, such as traffic intensity, industrial emissions, atmospheric mixing, wind, topography, and regional background pollution, likely contribute substantially to the remaining unexplained variation.

The results should therefore be interpreted cautiously. The land-cover variable is a proxy: it estimates the percentage of each PM2.5 polygon occupied by land uses that could plausibly contribute to fine particulate matter, but it does not directly measure emissions. The workflow is also affected by classification uncertainty, edge effects from Thiessen polygons, and the modifiable areal unit problem, because results may change under different polygon boundaries or raster resolutions.

A final limitation is the spatial resolution of the PM2.5 dataset itself. Although the Washington University surface is high quality for regional analysis and incorporates satellite observations, chemical transport modelling, and calibration to ground monitors, it still represents a gridded surface at about 1 km resolution. That means it is well suited to neighbourhood- and regional-scale comparison, but it may smooth fine-scale pollution gradients near individual roads, industrial parcels, or other localized sources.

Future Extensions

  • Use direct emissions, traffic count, or industrial facility data instead of only land-cover proxies.
  • Add meteorological and topographic variables to account for dispersion and accumulation.
  • Test multiple spatial resolutions to evaluate scale sensitivity and MAUP effects.
  • Compare results using alternative land-cover reclassification rules.
  • Use a multivariate spatial model if additional explanatory variables are available.
