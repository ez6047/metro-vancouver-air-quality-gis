PM2.5 and Potential Source Land Use at the 1 km Scale

An ArcGIS Pro analysis of whether land-cover classes that could plausibly contribute to PM2.5 are spatially associated with higher fine particulate matter concentrations across Metro Vancouver.

Course: GEOS 370 Software: ArcGIS Pro Study Area: Metro Vancouver

Project Snapshot

  • Remote-sensed PM2.5 raster surface
  • Land-cover raster reclassified into source/non-source categories
  • Thiessen polygon and tabulated-area workflow
  • OLS and GWR comparison with residual spatial autocorrelation testing

1. Home

Abstract

This project examines whether the percentage of land cover classified as a potential PM2.5 source is spatially associated with elevated PM2.5 concentrations across Metro Vancouver. The analysis uses a remote-sensed PM2.5 raster and a land-cover classification raster, both processed in ArcGIS Pro. Land-cover classes were reclassified into a binary source/non-source layer, while PM2.5 raster cells were converted into points and then Thiessen polygons so that surrounding land-cover composition could be summarized consistently.

The final modelling dataset was created by clipping PM2.5 polygons to the reclassified land-use polygon layer, tabulating area by class, calculating the percentage of each PM2.5 polygon covered by potential source land use, and joining that value back to the PM2.5 polygons. OLS and geographically weighted regression (GWR) were then used to compare the global and local spatial relationships.

2. Introduction

Urban Air Quality and Land-Cover Patterns

Air quality is a central urban environmental issue because it influences respiratory health, cardiovascular risk, and overall quality of life. Fine particulate matter, especially PM2.5, is commonly used as an indicator because these particles are small enough to remain suspended in air and penetrate deeply into the lungs.

Urban land-cover patterns can influence PM2.5 exposure because built surfaces, paved areas, industrial activity, transportation corridors, and other intensive land uses often cluster in particular parts of a metropolitan region. Metro Vancouver is a useful study area because it contains dense urban cores, suburban development, agricultural areas, industrial lands, water bodies, and large vegetated areas within a relatively compact region.

Why GIS for This Question?

GIS makes it possible to align raster and vector datasets, reclassify land-cover categories, summarize area by polygon, and visualize spatial patterns that would be difficult to interpret from tables alone.

4. Data

Input Datasets

PM2.5 Raster

PM2.5 refers to fine particulate matter with particle diameters less than 2.5 micrometres. The PM2.5 input is a remote-sensed raster dataset sourced from researchers at Washington University in St. Louis. It represents yearly mean PM2.5 concentration and has approximately 0.01° resolution, with grid cells roughly 1 km in length.

5. Methodology

ArcGIS Workflow

01

Reclassify Land Use

The land-cover raster was reclassified into binary categories: 0 for land-cover classes not treated as PM2.5 source areas and 1 for classes treated as potential PM2.5 source areas.

02

Convert Land Raster to Polygon

The reclassified land-use raster was converted to polygons so that source/non-source land-cover areas could be intersected and summarized within PM2.5 polygon units.

03

Create PM2.5 Thiessen Polygons

PM2.5 raster cells were converted to points. Thiessen polygons were then generated from those points to represent the area closest to each PM2.5 cell value.

04

Calculate Percent Source Land Use

PM2.5 polygons were clipped to the reclassified land-use polygons, tabulated by area, and used to calculate the percent of each polygon covered by potential source land use.

Detailed Workflow

  1. Reclassify the land-use raster into potential PM2.5 source and non-source categories.
  2. Convert the reclassified land-use raster to polygons.
  3. Convert the PM2.5 raster to points.
  4. Create Thiessen polygons from the PM2.5 points.
  5. Clip the PM2.5 polygons to the land-use polygons.
  6. Use Tabulate Area to calculate the total source and non-source land-use area within each PM2.5 polygon.
  7. Use Calculate Field to compute the percentage of each polygon classified as potential PM2.5 source land use.
  8. Join the tabulated-area table back to the clipped PM2.5 polygon layer.

6. Results

Maps and Model Outputs

Residual Spatial Autocorrelation

The OLS residuals show very strong positive spatial autocorrelation. The Global Moran’s I value is 0.957019, with a z-score of 76.098159 and a p-value of 0.000000. This means the OLS residuals are highly clustered rather than randomly distributed, so the global regression model does not fully account for the spatial structure in PM2.5.

The GWR residuals also remain spatially autocorrelated, but less strongly than the OLS residuals. The Global Moran’s I value decreases to 0.678473, with a z-score of 54.015470 and a p-value of 0.000000. This suggests that GWR captures some of the spatial non-stationarity in the relationship, but substantial spatial structure remains unexplained.

8. Discussion

Interpretation and Limitations

The analysis suggests that PM2.5 patterns are spatially structured and cannot be fully explained by a single global relationship with potential source land use. The OLS residuals are highly clustered, indicating that the global model leaves major spatial patterns unexplained. GWR reduces the strength of this residual clustering, which supports the interpretation that the relationship varies across Metro Vancouver rather than remaining constant across the full study area.

The results should be interpreted cautiously. The land-cover variable is a proxy: it estimates the percentage of each PM2.5 polygon occupied by land uses that could plausibly contribute to fine particulate matter, but it does not directly measure emissions. It also does not capture traffic counts, industrial emission rates, fuel type, wind, topography, temperature, atmospheric mixing, or regional background pollution.

A second limitation is the spatial resolution of the PM2.5 raster. The approximately 1 km scale is appropriate for regional comparison but may smooth fine-scale pollution gradients near roads, industrial parcels, or other localized sources. The workflow is also affected by classification uncertainty, edge effects from Thiessen polygons, and the modifiable areal unit problem because results may change under different polygon boundaries or raster resolutions.

Future Extensions

  • Use direct emissions, traffic count, or industrial facility data instead of only land-cover proxies.
  • Add meteorological and topographic variables to account for dispersion and accumulation.
  • Test multiple spatial resolutions to evaluate scale sensitivity and MAUP effects.
  • Compare results using alternative land-cover reclassification rules.
  • Use a multivariate spatial model if additional explanatory variables are available.
